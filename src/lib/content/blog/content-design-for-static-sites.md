When I first started working in law as a paralegal, a large part of my job was not related to giving advice but ensuring that the precedural steps throughout the matter were executed correctly. This work was given at the direction of the partner or senior associate. I learned a lot about what good advice looks like but also how to _deliver it_.

The procedural work looked like:

- completing tax forms
- preparing first drafts
- preparing client bundles accurately
- checking documents were executed correctly
- formatting (oh my god the formatting)

It wasn't all on me either, secretaries picked up dictation; the post team sealed, stamped and tracked outgoing mail; deeds clerks made sure document were stored and records updated. Looking back, everything worked.

_"Nostlagia is a helluva drug."_

In the first decade of my career, we aggressively dismantled the machine. Emails took over from post so lawyers typed their own (largely because they couldn't digure out delegate permissions in Outlook). Some documents could be digitally signed, or worse, signed-scanned-and-emailed back (yuck). The impact of email meant the cadence of inbound mail, review then reply got shorter and shorter. Client expectations rose too.

Then came instant messaging followed by client portals (most of which sucked). But the expectations went higher as everyone moved online. FinTech got a lot of this right with cleaner KYC processes, superior mobile apps and dashboards that mattered. Maybe the incentives helped there too with banks and others driving down costs and benefiting from speed - the same incentives don't always sit well with law firms (we'll come back to this). My own frustrations were boiling over.

About 10 years ago I made a conscious choice to pivot into legal engineering. It wasn't very well defined then but there were pockets of enthusiasm, curiosity and a growing number of stubborn souls who wanted to try. I started going to events where developers hung out both in real life and online to understand how machines are built in a digital world. Between coding on my lunchbreaks and setting up my own document automation instances, I got excited enough to write about [Why Lawyers Should Make Good Developers](https://medium.datadriveninvestor.com/why-lawyers-should-make-great-developers-155f73304d0) in 2018.

A little while later I landed my first role as a legal engineer and continued building.
